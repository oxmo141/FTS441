The event is designed to focus on the vehicle’s suspension design characteristics and tune-ability for maximum lateral grip and minimise the effect of driver reflexes during transitional manoeuvres (Goslawski, Snyder, 2023).


Skidpad performance can be evaluated on the G-G plot of the vehicle's lateral acceleration against longitudinal acceleration during the event. The skidpad tests the vehicle's steady-state cornering capabilities, which is represented by minimal variance in lateral acceleration throughout the run. This indicates that the vehicle was unable to maintain a consistent cornering force, loosing lap time potential and indicating handling issues.

2. Suspension Design Optimisation

Dynamic behaviour of a vehicle can be described by 2 main terms: Roadholding and Handling. Roadholding refers to the vehicle's ability to maintain contact with the road surface, by controlling lifting movements along the vertical axis (z-axis), such as heave, or a rotational movement around the lateral axis (y-axis), such as pitch (Pütz, Serné, 2025). Handling, on the other hand, refers to the lateral dynamicis and thus describes the ability of a vehicle to drive around a corner quickly and stably (Pütz, Serné, 2025).


2.1. Suspension Stiffness

R25e ran a high suspension spring stiffness rate of 300 lb/in (52.4 N/mm) at both axles. High spring rates are typically used to reduce body roll and improve responsiveness during cornering, sacrificing the vehicle's roadholding capabilities. Typically for a FSAE Michigan competition track, the conditions are smooth and well-maintained, allowing for the use of stiffer suspension setups. However, in the case of R25e, the high spring rates may have contributed to the handling issues. To better understand the impact of suspension stiffness, I will be analysing on suspension roll dynamics.


2.1.1. What Happens During Roll?


During cornering, the centrifugal force acting on the vehicle center of gravity causes the vehicle to roll about its roll center. This roll motion results in the compression of the suspension on the outer wheels (bump travel) and the extension of the suspension on the inner wheels (droop travel). Once the limit of suspension travel is reached, the inner wheel will lift off the ground, resulting in a loss of traction and stability.

2.1.2. Droop and Bump Travel Analysis


After performing calculations on the suspension spring compression. It was found that the 300 lb/in suspension springs were only compressing by 10.223 mm under static loading conditions. That results in a 10.223 mm available droop travel and a remaining 46.777 mm of bump travel. The total stroke is 57 mm, which is the maximum travel of the Ohlins TTX25 MKll shock absorbers used in R25e and R26e.


The data above represents the damper travel of the left and right rear tyres during the competition skidpad event. From the plot, droop travel is represented by positive damper travel values, while bump travel is represented by negative damper travel values. It is evident that the damper travels an estimated maximum of 8 mm in bump travel while it can only travel a maximum of 6 mm in droop travel. Not much can be inferred from this plot since the car was not driven hard enough to visualise any anomalies in the damper travel. Hence, I will be analysing the damper travel during autocross, where the car was driven more aggressively.


From the competition autocross data above, we can see that the droop travel for both left and right rear tyres is blocked by an invisible line at 6 mm of damper travel, whereas the bump travel is hitting an estimated maximum of 12 mm of damper travel. This indicates that the suspension droop travel limit is at 6 mm, and the limit of the damper travel has been hit for both autocross and skidpad events. This is a clear indication that the suspension setup is too stiff, causing the inner wheel to lift off the ground during cornering as the damper is unable to extend any further.

2.2. Final Conclusion - Suspension Stiffness

This is a tricky topic to conclude, as the trade-offs of changing suspension stiffness are very significant to the handling and roadholding characteristics of the vehicle. From the analysis above, it is evident that the inner tyre is lifting off the ground during cornering due to the limited droop travel of the suspension.


In theory when dynamic load changes occur, it is expected that the overall lateral grip of the axle will decrease due to the non-linear characteristics of tyre grip. As shown in the image above, when a 4 kN of load difference occurs, the lateral force at that axle decreases against the ideal average lateral force when there is no load difference between left and right tyre. For a racing car, it is expected to keep the wheel load changes that occur as low as possible to achieve the best driving performance (Frömming, 2023). When the inner wheel lifts off the ground, it results in 100% load transfer on that axle.

In rally cars, it is common practice to lift the inner rear wheel off the ground during hard cornering. For a Front Wheel Drive (FWD) car, this technique is used to maximise the traction of the driven wheels by lifting the inner rear wheel off the ground, allowing the outer rear wheel to overload and yaw the car. However, for a Rear Wheel Drive (RWD) car like R25e and R26e, it is unclear the effects of lifting the inner rear wheel off the ground.

For the R26e, the suspension spring rates will be reduced to 225 lb/in (39.3 N/mm) at the front and 200 lb/in (34.9 N/mm) at the rear. This change is expected to increase the droop travel of the suspension, allowing for more droop travel allowance for dynamic events. For the difference in spring rates, the front suspension will be stiffer for more turn-in response in corners and control oversteer characteristics during bumps. The rear suspension will be relatively softer to increase mechanical grip and minimise contact patch variation, which is important for the driven wheels. However, the drawbacks of softening both front and rear axles are increased body roll during cornering and reduced responsiveness, which changes the handling of the car drastically.

Thorough testing and validation are required before we can assess whether this modification effectively minimises lateral acceleration variations observed during the skidpad event and improves vehicle handling performance.

2.3. Steering Torque Analysis


The steering assembly can be analysed from the rack and pinion mechanism above. The steering torque from the driver overcomes the resistive torque from the tyre-road interaction and the frictional losses in the steering system.


The steering torque equation above represents the resistive load that the driver must overcome to maintain a constant steering angle in a corner. From the equation, we can see that the resistive torque is directly proportional to the generalised steering ratio (i). This value (i) is inversely proportional to the steering ratio (SR) of the vehicle, which is defined as the ratio between the steering wheel angle and the road wheel angle. The other components of the equation represent the tyre forces and geometry.


From Table 2, I will be focusing on the steering ratio (SR) and the King-Pin Inclination angle (σ) as the main factors that changed between R25e and R26e. 2.3.1. Steering Ratio (SR)

From Table 2, the steering ratio (SR) of R26e is higher than R25e, increasing from 4.577 to 4.902 on the inner wheel, and from 4.644 to 6.213 on the outer wheel. This change in steering ratio results in a decrease in the generalised steering ratio (i), leading to a reduction in the resistive steering torque that the driver must overcome.


The steering ratio (SR) is obtained from the OptimumKinematics software, which is used for suspension and steering kinematics. The plot above in red is the steering ratio of R26e which is greater than the steering ratio of the R25e. The graph is plotted over a range of steering motion from 0° to 120° steering wheel input and the steering ratio is averaged over the left and right wheels.

2.3.2. King-Pin Inclination angle (σ)

Apart from steering ratio, the King-Pin Inclination angle (σ), also plays a significant role in the steering resistive torque. From Table 1, we can see that the King-Pin Inclination angle (σ) has decreased from 11.7° in R25e to 8° in R26e.



The vertical force component (Fz) acting on lateral inclination angle/ King-Pin Inclination angle (σ), results in a sine angle force component (Fz x sinσ), which nominally acts laterally on the moment arm, scrub radius (s) multiplied by sine of steering angle (sinδ). This moment is initially zero at zero steering angle and increases the resistive torque as the steering angle increases (Gillespie, 2021).

From a suspension geometry perspective, introducing high King-Pin Inclination angle (σ) will increase the lift or jacking effect during cornering on both tyres (Derek, 2015). This jacking effect is felt by the driver as an additional steering torque needed to lift an effective component of the vehicle's weight as steering angle increases.

2.4. Final Conclusion - Steering Torque Analysis

For this iteration of R26e, I aimed for a steering ratio (SR) that is approximately in the range of R25e and R24e to balance between steering responsiveness and steering effort. From the comments of the drivers over the three batches, despite the fatigue from the endurance event, they were content with the steering responsiveness due to the high steering ratio (SR) implemented.

For R26e, a different approach was taken to raise Ackermann percentage by inducing a higher difference in steering ratio (SR) between the inner and outer wheels. The effect of this change is uncertain until testing and validation is done, but it is expected to improve the turn-in response of the FSAE car during cornering as the Ackermann effect is significant in racing series with low cornering speeds (Milliken, Milliken, 1995).

As for King-Pin Inclination angle (σ), I am addressing the heavy steering feedback at low speeds. Corners that require low speeds tend to be hairpins in the FSAE track that require significant steering input. By reducing the King-Pin Inclination angle (σ), the jacking effect during cornering is reduced, leading to a lighter steering effort for the driver.

All in all, after calculating the steering moment needed to overcome the resistive torque from the tyre-road interaction, the normalised steering torque for R26e is approximately 77.3% of R25e. The assumptions made are that the tyre forces and moments remain the same between R25e and R26e, which is reasonable since both cars used the same tyre compound and size. However, the accuracy can be improved by conducting physical testing and improving our tyre model to produce combined slip characteristics that are more representative of a real FSAE car.

The biggest change was the steering ratio (SR), which will reduce the steering responsiveness of the car. The trade-off is worth the risk to improve the precision of driver inputs during long stints.

By addressing the steering feedback, I am directly reducing driver fatigue during long endurance stints. This will allow the driver to maintain better control over the car and make more precise inputs, leading to improved handling and lap times.
